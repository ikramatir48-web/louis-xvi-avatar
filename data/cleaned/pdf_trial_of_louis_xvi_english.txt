THE TRIAL OF LOUIS XVI, LATE KING OF FRANCE, WHO WAS BEHEADED ON MONDAY, JANUARY 21, 1793.

On December 3, 1792, M. Barbaroux demanded that the fate of Louis XVI be decided. Robespierre opposed any formal trial process, arguing it was a counter-revolutionary idea: "I propose to decide instantly upon the fate of Louis XVI... For Louis I demand that the National Convention shall declare him a traitor to the French nation, and a criminal to humanity."

The Convention decreed that Louis XVI would be judged by the Convention itself, and would proceed with the trial daily from eleven o'clock till six.

ACT OF ACCUSATION (December 11, 1792)

The accused, addressed as "Louis Capet," was brought before the bar of the Convention. The President read the charges: attacking the sovereignty of the people on June 20, 1789; marching troops against citizens of Paris; the flight to Varennes; collusion with Lafayette and foreign powers; the massacre of citizens on August 10.

To each charge, Louis responded with calm and brevity. To the accusation regarding the events of August 10 (the storming of the Tuileries), he stated: "I went to see the troops who were assembled round me on that day; the constitutional authorities were with me." When asked about bloodshed, he replied simply: "No, sir, it was not I."

Louis requested legal counsel: "I demand a communication of the accusations which I have heard, and the liberty of choosing a counsel to defend me." This was granted by the Convention.

THE DEFENSE (January 4, 1793)

Louis's counsel included Malesherbes, Tronchet, and de Sèze. M. de Sèze delivered an extended defense, arguing that the Constitution of 1791 had granted the king inviolability, and that the only constitutional penalty for any wrongdoing would have been forfeiture of the throne — which had already occurred. He argued that trying the king under new, retroactively-applied standards violated basic principles of justice: "I search among you for judges, and I see only accusers."

At the conclusion, Louis himself addressed the Convention: "Citizens, the means of my defense have been just exposed to you, I shall not resume them. In speaking to you, perhaps for the last time, I declare that my conscience reproaches me with nothing... My heart is torn when I perceive myself suspected of having wished to shed the blood of the people."

THE VOTING (January 15-19, 1793)

Three questions were put to a roll-call vote of the Convention:
1. Is Louis guilty of conspiracy against liberty and the general safety of the state? — 691 voted guilty, with 10 abstentions.
2. Should the verdict be submitted to ratification by the people (appeal to the people)? — Rejected by 424 to 287.
3. What punishment should be applied?

During the third vote, many deputies gave individual statements. Robespierre: "I vote for the punishment of death." Thomas Paine voted for banishment rather than death, citing France's alliance with America, which had benefited from Louis's support during its revolution. Condorcet and others argued for delay or imprisonment.

The final tally for execution without delay carried by a narrow majority: 361 votes for immediate death, against votes for imprisonment, banishment, or death with a delayed/conditional sentence. A subsequent vote on whether to grant a reprieve (sursis) was defeated 380 to 310 — a margin of 70 votes.

Louis's lawyers protested the narrowness of repeated votes and the irregularity of the proceedings, but the Convention proceeded to confirm the sentence of death, to be executed within 24 hours.

THE EXECUTION (January 21, 1793)

According to the official report of the Commune of Paris: Louis spent his final evening with his family for an emotional farewell lasting nearly two hours. The following morning, Louis rose at five o'clock, heard Mass from his confessor (Henry Essex Edgeworth), and prepared himself calmly.

At half past eight, a guard of 1,200 horsemen arrived to escort the former king on a two-hour carriage ride to the place of execution. Edgeworth accompanied him in the carriage. Louis spent the journey reciting psalms from the priest's breviary in silence.

Upon arrival at the Place de la Révolution, Louis descended from the carriage. When guards attempted to bind his hands, he initially resisted: "What are you attempting?" said the King, drawing back his hands. "To bind you," answered the guards. "To bind me," said the King, with an indignant air. "No! I shall never consent to that." His confessor persuaded him to submit.

Louis mounted the scaffold with firmness, and in a voice loud enough to be heard widely, pronounced: "I die innocent of all the crimes laid to my charge; I pardon those who have occasioned my death; and I pray to God that the blood you are going to shed may never be visited on France."

He was interrupted by drums beaten on command before he could finish speaking further. The blade fell. The youngest of the guards present, about eighteen years old, seized the severed head and displayed it to the crowd. After an initial silence, cries of "Vive la République!" spread through the assembled multitude.

LAST WILL AND TESTAMENT OF LOUIS XVI (as published in this English edition)

In the name of the Holy Trinity, Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. This 25th of December, 1792, I, Louis, sixteenth of the name, King of France, imprisoned in the Temple for more than four months by those who were my subjects, involved in a trial of which the outcome cannot be known from the passions of men, declare my sentiments.

I leave my soul to God, my creator, praying him not to judge it according to my merits but those of Jesus Christ. I forgive those who have become my enemies and pray God to forgive them likewise.

I commend to God my wife, my children, and my sister. I recommend my children to my wife, and entreat her to watch over their education. I recommend to my son, if he ever becomes king, to think only of the happiness of his fellow citizens, mindful that he can only be happy by ruling according to law.

I protest that I am not guilty of any of the crimes with which I am reproached.

This will was sent by the Commune to the Executive Power.
