This academic work examines Louis XVI's role as constitutional monarch between 1789 and 1792, a period often overlooked compared to the dramatic events of his trial and execution.

In 1801, the abbé Jean-Louis Soulavie published a major history of Louis XVI's reign in six volumes, based on access to the king's private papers found at Versailles and the Tuileries after the fall of the monarchy. Soulavie argued that the "fleeting and uncertain character of Louis XVI is the primary cause of the collapse of the ancient monarchy and also of the fall of the constitutional monarchy." This portrait of an essentially well-intentioned but indecisive monarch, unable to choose between asserting royal will and following public opinion, became influential in later historical interpretations.

Modern biographers continue to debate Louis XVI's character. John Hardman, a prominent English biographer, notes that Louis regarded his actions during the constitutional monarchy period (1789-1791) as provisional, since as he stated in a declaration he left behind before his flight to Varennes, his "palace was a prison" and promises made under duress were not binding.

THE TRANSITION FROM ANCIEN RÉGIME COURT TO CONSTITUTIONAL MONARCHY

On 17 June 1789 the Third Estate declared itself to be the National Assembly, creating a dual and competing form of political authority alongside the king. From this moment, the legitimacy of royal government institutions was continually contested.

The royal court under Louis XVI maintained much of its ancien régime ceremonial routine even after the forced move to the Tuileries Palace in Paris on 6 October 1789. This persistence of traditional representational culture during a time of crisis reflected both the king's genuine commitment to his ancestors' traditions and his distrust of revolutionary innovations.

Louis XVI was known to have little interest in lavish entertainments, dancing, or gambling — traits noted even by his own contemporaries. However, he remained scrupulously attached to ceremony itself, seeing it as connected to the dignity and glory of his royal house, in keeping with Bourbon tradition.

THE KING'S CONSTITUTIONAL POSITION

The new constitutional framework established after 1789 redefined the monarch as an agent of the sovereign nation rather than a sovereign in his own right. Radical deputies and journalists increasingly referred to Louis as "premier mandataire" or "fonctionnaire de l'état" (the first civil servant of the state) — a definition Louis found deeply offensive, conflicting with his conviction, instilled since youth, that he had been divinely appointed to rule.

The Civil Constitution of the Clergy, promulgated in 1790, proved to be a major turning point that polarized politics and made the monarchy's continued attachment to traditional Catholic ritual and practice appear not merely unpopular but scandalous to revolutionary opinion.

Following the flight to Varennes in June 1791, the wording of the final 1791 Constitution was carefully adjusted to avoid referring to the king as a mere civil servant, stating instead that executive power was "delegated to the king to be exercised under his authority" — though this concession to royalist sensibilities did not satisfy public opinion, which remained increasingly suspicious of any display of royal independence or pageantry.

CONCLUSION OF THE INTRODUCTION

The central argument is that the assault on the ancien régime monarchy had its origins in the earliest days of the Revolution, but the impossibility of accommodating the crown within a regenerated French state only became fully evident once the Civil Constitution of the Clergy passed into law, and was made inevitable by the declaration of war on Austria and Prussia in 1792. The king's persistence in maintaining traditional court ceremony, regardless of whether he was engaged in any double-dealing with émigrés or foreign powers, was interpreted negatively by both the Assembly and public opinion — ultimately contributing to the radicalization of politics and the collapse of the very monarchy he sought to preserve.
